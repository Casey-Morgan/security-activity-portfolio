# A16. Discover 3 local security incidents

## Description
For this activity, I identified three local security incidents connected to Western Australia. These cases show that cyber and information security incidents can affect universities, public services, and the wider community.

## Selected incidents

### 1. University of Western Australia data breach
On 11 August 2025, ABC News reported that the University of Western Australia was investigating a cyber attack that exposed the password information of thousands of staff and students. UWA locked accounts, reset passwords, and said there was no evidence that other data had been accessed at that stage.

Source: https://www.abc.net.au/news/2025-08-11/university-of-western-australia-uwa-suffers-major-data-breach/105636074

### 2. University of Notre Dame cyber attack in Perth
On 19 February 2025, ABC News reported that the University of Notre Dame in Perth was still dealing with disruption weeks after a ransomware attack. The incident affected IT systems, disrupted enrolment and timetable access, and forced the university to rely on manual processes while it worked to verify claims that data may also have been accessed.

Source: https://www.abc.net.au/news/2025-02-19/university-of-notre-dame-cyber-attack/104955012

### 3. WA public health data breach
On 16 October 2025, ABC News published a report about one of Western Australia’s biggest public health data breaches. The report said sensitive hospital-related information, including patient and staff names, contact details, and communications, had been exposed after unencrypted frequencies were intercepted and the decoded data was uploaded to a website.

Source: https://www.abc.net.au/news/2025-10-17/joshua-unwittingly-triggered-major-wa-health-data-breach-autism/105455250

## Summary and comparison of the incidents

These three local incidents show that security problems in Western Australia can affect both education and public health. The UWA incident involved unauthorised access to password information for thousands of staff and students, but the university said there was no evidence that other data had been accessed. The Notre Dame incident was a ransomware attack that disrupted enrolment, timetables, coursework access, and other university processes. The WA public health case involved the exposure of sensitive patient and staff information after unencrypted radio communications were intercepted and uploaded to a website. 

A main similarity is that all three incidents affected important local institutions and created risks to sensitive information or essential services. A main difference is the type of impact. The UWA case was mainly about credential exposure and account security. The Notre Dame case had a stronger operational impact because university systems were disrupted for weeks. The WA health case was more directly a privacy breach because sensitive communications and personal details were exposed. 

Another important comparison is the cause of the incidents. The UWA and Notre Dame cases were cyber attacks against university systems, while the WA health case was caused by poor protection of radio communications rather than a traditional hacking attack. This shows that local security incidents do not always come from the same source. Some are direct attacks, while others happen because sensitive information was not protected properly in the first place.

Overall, these incidents show that local organisations need both strong cyber defences and good protection of sensitive data. They also show that incident response matters because even when the causes are different, the consequences can include disruption, privacy harm, and loss of trust. 


## Reflection
This activity showed that local security incidents can affect education, healthcare, and public trust. It also showed that security is not only about stopping attackers, but also about protecting sensitive information, responding quickly, and reducing harm when something goes wrong.
